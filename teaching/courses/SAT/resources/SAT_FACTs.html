From Perterson's Guide

Who should take the SAT?

You, if you are reading this! High school students generally take the SAT in the spring of their junior year or the fall of their senior year in order to meet application deadlines. Since scores tend to improve with each year of schooling, you should not take the SAT before junior year. However, you also shouldn't take the SAT at the last possible minute. That way, you can take it again if you are disappointed in your score.

Since many schools accept both the ACT and the SAT, you may be wondering which you should take. Both are curriculum-based tests; they attempt to measure what you know in a variety of subjects. Both the SAT and the ACT test critical reading skills, writing skills, and math; only the ACT tests science. The SAT is more common on the east and west coasts, while the ACT is more common in the middle of the country. Neither test is "better" or "easier" than the other; you should take the test with which you feel more comfortable.

 

What should I expect to see on the SAT?

Format

The SAT consists of 10 sections and will take 3 hours and 45 minutes to complete, including breaks. There are 3 Critical Reading sections, 3 Math sections, 3 Writing sections, and one experimental section that could be Critical Reading, Math, or Writing, although it will not be an essay. The experimental section will not be scored.

The Writing Sections

One Writing section will require you to write an essay in 25 minutes. You will be asked to write about a specific topic, which could be on any subject, but it will not require specialized knowledge. It will be graded based on how clearly you express your ideas, how well you structure your essay, and how accurately you use language.

The other two Writing sections (one is 25 minutes long and one is 10 minutes long) consist of three types of multiple-choice questions. Error Identification questions ask you to find the error, if there is one, in a sentence. Sentence Improvement questions ask you to select the best version of an underlined portion of a sentence. Paragraph Improvement questions ask you to read an essay and answer questions about it. Most of the questions are about how the essay is organized or how the ideas are developed.

The Critical Reading Sections

Two Critical Reading sections are 25 minutes long and one is 20 minutes long. These sections consist of Sentence Completions and passage-based reading comprehension questions. All questions are multiple choice.

Sentence Completions are sentences that contain a blank or blanks in place of a word or words; you have to use the context clues and your knowledge of vocabulary to determine which of the words in the answer choices best fits in the sentence.

Reading comprehension questions are based on passages given in the sections. These questions will ask about vocabulary, the structure of the passage, the writer's purpose for writing, details in the passage, the main idea of the passage, and the author’s writing style. Some passages will be short (about a paragraph in length), while others will be long (about 4-6 paragraphs in length). There will also be sets of paired short passages and paired long passages. Questions that follow these sets may ask about both passages individually and may ask you to compare and contrast both passages.

The Math Sections

Two Math sections are 25 minutes long and one is 20 minutes long. One of the 25 minute sections contains some multiple-choice questions and some student-produced response questions (also called grid-ins). The student-produced response questions have no answer choices; you must determine the answer on your own and enter it into the grid provided. The other two Math sections contain all multiple-choice questions. Topics tested in the Math sections include numbers and operations, arithmetic, algebra, geometry, and data analysis.

 

The Five Elements of an Essay

There is no strict formula for writing a good SAT essay. You may write an essay that is narrative, expository, persuasive, or argumentative. To support your ideas, you may draw on a wide base of knowledge: from the classroom, from literature outside of school, from extracurricular activities, from current events, and from personal observation or experience.

Essay readers do not grade based on whether they agree with your opinion. Readers do, however, grade based on whether you write on the given topic. An essay that does not address the prompt receives a score of 0.

You are not expected to write a polished essay in 25 minutes. Readers grade based primarily on the five elements of an essay, detailed in the table below. If these elements are presented effectively, the essay will score high. If one or more of these elements are flawed, the reader will deduct points.

Development and Support:

• how fully the essay responds to the prompt.

• the essay’s sense of completeness.

• the essay’s focus on the issue and avoidance of “filler.”

• the quality and sufficiency of examples supporting the writer’s position.

• the depth of critical thinking and reasoning.

Organization:

• the order of sentences and paragraphs.

• the use of effective transitions.

• the flow of ideas from the essay’s introduction to its body through to its conclusion.

Language:

• how accurately words and phrases communicate the author’s ideas.

• how well the author varies word choice.

• the level of vocabulary the author displays.

Sentence Structure:

• how well the author uses a variety of sentence types that are correctly punctuated.

• how well and often the author varies sentence structure in meaningful and purposeful ways.

Conventions:

• how correctly the author uses punctuation (commas, apostrophes, periods, colons, etc.).

• the author’s correctness in grammar and mechanics (subject/ verb agreement, verb tense, subject/pronoun agreement, etc.).

SAT Essay Scoring Guide

Essays are graded holistically. This means that while you should keep all of the five elements in mind as you read, you should score your essay based on an overall impression. Small errors of fact, spelling, or grammar should not cause you to deduct points.

On the official SAT, two readers independently mark each essay to ensure a fair and accurate score. Each reader assigns a score from 0 to 6, yielding a combined score from 0 to 12. In the event that the readers’ scores differ by more than one point, a third reader gives the decisive score. This score is then doubled to get the final combined score.

For this test, you will grade your own essay. After reading the essay and considering how effectively it demonstrates each of the five elements, assign it a score from 0 to 6. The information below explains what these numbers mean.

Score of 6

• The essay fully develops a point of view on the issue, showing high-level critical thinking and using appropriate and ample evidence.

• Its organization and focus are apparent throughout.

• It demonstrates a skillful use of language and sentence structure.

• It is free or mostly free of errors.

Score of 5

• The essay develops a point of view on the issue, showing critical thinking and generally using appropriate and ample evidence.

• It demonstrates organization and focus.

• It contains appropriately varied language and sentence structure.

• It is generally free of errors.

Score of 4

• The essay develops a point of view on the issue, showing some critical thinking and generally using adequate evidence.

• It is somewhat focused and has a somewhat organized progression of ideas.

• It inconsistently uses varied language and sentence structure.

• It has some errors.

Score of 3

• The essay develops a point of view on the issue, showing some critical thinking but using inadequate evidence.

• It is limited in its organization, with some flaws in the progression of ideas.

•It demonstrates some weaknesses or errors in vocabulary and sentence structure.

• It has several errors.

Score of 2

• The essay only partially develops a point of view on the issue, showing weak critical thinking and using inadequate evidence.

• It is poorly organized.

• It demonstrates a limited vocabulary and errors in sentence structure.

• It has serious errors that confuse meaning.

Score of 1

• The essay fails to develop a point of view on the issue or use appropriate evidence.

• It is unorganized.

• It demonstrates multiple errors in word choice and sentence structure.

• It has serious errors that interfere with meaning.

Where can I register for the SAT?

There are several ways to register for the SAT. If you wish to register by mail (which is required of test takers with special needs), you need to obtain a Registration Bulletin from your school counselor's office. If you do not need any special accommodations, you can register online at the test maker's Web site: www.collegeboard.com. If you have already taken the SAT and are reregistering, you may register over the telephone by calling 1-866-756-7346.

Why do colleges require the SAT?

Colleges look at many different factors when comparing applicants. Transcripts, class rank, teacher recommendations, essays, and interviews all play an important role in evaluating applications. But how well can colleges compare grades that were earned at different schools? Maybe the same work that earned a C+ at your high school would have been graded as an A- at the school in the next district. Colleges use the SAT because it provides a standard way to compare all applicants. Everyone takes the same test, so colleges know how to interpret score reports. This is not to say that the SAT is an objective measure of intelligence or ability; everyone knows very intelligent people who simply "don't test well." That's why colleges look at your SAT score as just one part of your application among many.

How is the SAT scored, and how can I improve my score?

Score reports can be confusing. There are raw scores, scaled scores, and percentile ranks, each of which is calculated separately for Critical Reading, Writing, and Math. Here's a brief summary of how these scores are calculated and what they mean:
 

Raw Score

Your raw score is calculated first. 1 point is added for each correct answer, and a fraction of a point is subtracted for each incorrect answer (except for incorrect Student Produced Responses, which carry no penalty and the Essay, which is scored as a whole). A question that is left blank neither adds to nor subtracts from your raw score.

Scaled Score

Your scaled score is then computed from your raw score using a statistical process that accounts for slight differences between different versions of the test. The scores on each of the three portions of the test range from 200 to 800, giving you a total combined score range of 600 to 2400. The score report also gives a score range, which indicates the range of scores you might expect to receive if you were to take the SAT several times.

Percentiles

Your percentile rank tells you how well you did compared to other students who recently took the SAT. For example, if your national percentile is 56, then you scored better than 56 percent of the students who took the SAT nationally. You are given a percentile for the nation and a percentile for your state, which may differ based on the performance of these two groups.

Maximizing Your Score

To maximize your score, you should familiarize yourself with the format of the test and the content covered, and you should practice some general test-taking tips. You can visit www.petersons.com for extensive SAT preparation, but here are some basic tips for the meantime.

1) Keep a steady pace.
 

Pacing is important. Spending too much time on any one question will keep you from scoring points on other questions. More difficult questions will take somewhat more time, but remember that all points are created equal: getting an easy question right improves your score as much as getting a hard question right. Make sure that the time you spend on a question is worth the point.

2) Make educated guesses.

Statistically, you will improve your score by guessing when you can eliminate at least one answer choice. If you can't eliminate any answer choices, you should leave the question blank; but if you can eliminate even one choice, then you should guess from among the remaining choices.

3) Stay focused.

It's a long test, and your attention is bound to wander. If you find that you are becoming distracted, look away from the test for a few seconds and center yourself. Taking those few seconds to breathe and relax will help you score more points when you return to the test.

4) Calm down.

Your state of mind is critical to your performance on standardized tests. When you are taking the test, don't think about college admissions or anything like that: just stay focused on the task at hand, which is answering the questions to the best of your ability. Nervousness distracts you and prevents you from maximizing your score.
